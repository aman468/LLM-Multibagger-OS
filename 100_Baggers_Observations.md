## Core Philosophy

Investing is a probabilistic bet against what lies ahead. Beyond numbers, it is about understanding a business and its prospects so deeply that you can make outsized, concentrated bets on it. When the expected payoff probability is high relative to maximum payoff, you must double down. A few large, infrequent bets — that is the nature of finding 100 baggers.

A business that can grow **and** create economic value simultaneously is rare. But you don't need many of them to build wealth. A few great ideas a year is all you need, provided you don't chicken out on position sizing once you've identified them.

## The 5 Characteristics of a Potential 100 Bagger

### 1. Catch Them Young
Find companies before the crowd does. If you discover the beauty of a stock after it's already celebrated, you're just one more fan late to the party. The best entry is early — when the business is still unknown. A second chance may come during periods of fear and capitulation (dry powder + courage), but only if the company has manageable debt and the resilience to survive the crisis.

### 2. Profitable Growth + High ROIC (The Twin Engines)
Growth is a **necessary but not sufficient** condition for a 100 bagger. You want growth that **creates economic value** — not toxic growth that destroys it.

The second engine: the ability to **reinvest profits at a rate higher than the cost of capital** (high ROIC) for a long, sustained period.

What sustains high ROIC? **Competitive Advantage — SCIND:**
- **S** — Switching Costs (costly for customers to leave)
- **C** — Cost Advantage
- **I** — Intangible Assets (brands, patents, licenses, IP)
- **N** — Network Effect
- **D** — Distribution Advantage

### 3. Skin in the Game (Owner-Operator Alignment)
Prefer businesses where the **agency problem is absent or minimal**. Owner-operators or management whose financial incentives are tightly aligned with minority shareholders. High promoter/insider holding is a strong positive signal.

### 4. Quality of Management (Capital Allocation)
Management must consistently make **right capital allocation decisions** — reinvesting earnings at high rates when opportunities are plentiful. Even the best business cannot overcome a management team that destroys capital. This is why filtering for profitable, high-ROIC businesses is the starting point.

### 5. GARP — Growth at a Reasonable Price
Many great companies exist, but you cannot achieve 100-bagger returns if you overpay. **Bhav Bhagwaan Che** (Price is God). Margin of safety matters. Valuation is not optional.

### 6. Kelly Criterion — Position Sizing
Once you've found a genuine 100-bagger candidate, **make a meaningful allocation**. Identifying the right stock but under-allocating defeats the entire purpose of the research effort.

## The Research Process

### Why Apply Filters?
You don't have the bandwidth to search all 5,178 listed companies in India. The goal of filtering is to eliminate noise (bhusa) without eliminating the needle (sui). Wrong filters are as dangerous as no filters.

### Starting Universe (India, as of Oct 2025)
| Filter | Companies Remaining |
|--------|-------------------|
| All listed companies | 5,178 |
| Market Cap > ₹1,000 Cr (trust in financials) | 1,515 |
| 0 < PEG < 2.5 | 739 |
| ROIC > 15% | 474 |
| Promoter Holding > 40% | ~350–400 (estimated) |

---

## Filter Details

### Filter 1: PEG Ratio (0 < PEG < 2.5)
**What it addresses:** GARP (growth at reasonable price) + profitability screen.

**What it filters out:**
- Loss-making companies (negative earnings → no PEG)
- Overvalued growth stories
- New-age companies growing revenue but not earnings
- Companies with EPS dilution (growing shares outstanding)

**Blind spots:** Filters out quality companies in cyclical troughs or temporary setbacks. Re-run filters during market downturns — great companies reappear at lower PE multiples.

**Time estimate:** At 15 min/company × 739 companies = ~185 hours. At 3 hours/day ≈ 2 months of manual filtering.

---

### Filter 2: ROIC > 15%
**What it addresses:** Competitive advantage + management quality (points 2 and 4).

**Why 15% and not higher?** To avoid filtering out young companies or turnaround stories that show early promise. Multi-year average ROCE is deliberately avoided as it penalizes newer businesses.

---

### Filter 3: Promoter Holding > 40%
**What it addresses:** Skin in the game (point 3). High insider alignment reduces agency risk.

## Principles for Holding

- **Do not sell on price volatility.** Stock price noise is not business news.
- **The sell criterion must be met on first principles** — business deterioration, valuation absurdity, or a fundamentally better opportunity.
- **Buy right and sit tight.** Compounding rewards patience, not activity.
- **Patience is the edge.** Most investors exit prematurely. Your advantage is staying put.

---

## Mindset Rules

**Rule #1:** Do not stop looking for 100 baggers. There aren't many — that's the point. You don't need many. Handful is enough.

**Rule #2:** Before you find something, know exactly what you are looking for. Understand what it looks like, where to find it, and how to distinguish the real thing from a lookalike.

**Rule #3:** Numbers point you in the right direction, but the ultimate investment decision requires deep understanding of business dynamics. Don't over-rely on quantitative filters. Don't bother predicting macro. Focus your limited bandwidth on understanding the business model and industry dynamics.

## Summary (Author's Key Takeaways)

1. Identify characteristics of potential 100 baggers: young companies, growth potential, economic value creation, management quality, owner-operator alignment, reasonable price.
2. Use filters to narrow investment candidates: PEG ratio, ROIC, promoter holding.
3. Focus on understanding the business and its dynamics — not macro predictions.
4. Patience is crucial; avoid reacting to stock price volatility.
5. Historical data shows only a small percentage of stocks become 100 baggers.
6. Knowledge and skill significantly increase the probability of selecting successful investments.
